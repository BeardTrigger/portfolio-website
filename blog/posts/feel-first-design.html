"Feel First" Design: Building wAv Around an Emotion

How I approached designing wAv by starting with a target feeling rather than a mechanic — and why that order of operations changed everything.

🌊

Most games start with a mechanic. What if you could do X? It's a natural way to think — mechanics are concrete, prototypable, and easy to communicate. But when I started on wAv, I deliberately broke that habit.

The question I started with instead: What do I want the player to feel?

Defining the target emotion

I wanted wAv to feel like a Sunday morning. Calm, unhurried, but quietly satisfying. Not a game you grind — a game you settle into. That sounds fuzzy and undesignable, but it turns out a clear emotional target is one of the most useful tools you can have.

Every design decision has a filter: does this serve the feeling? A timer? No — timers create anxiety. A combo multiplier? No — that creates urgency. A gentle ripple that nudges objects? Yes — that's calm agency.

When mechanics follow feeling

The wave mechanic didn't come first — the feeling did. Once I knew I wanted the player to feel like they were gently influencing a serene system rather than controlling it, the wave metaphor was obvious. You don't push objects, you create a disturbance that reaches them. You're not in control — you're in conversation with the environment.

"The best mechanics in this kind of game should feel discovered, not imposed."

This applies beyond puzzle games. On RTG, the emotional target of "earning your legacy" shaped mechanical decisions too — why some milestones feel ceremonial rather than just numerical, why certain moments get extra camera treatment.

The limits of feel-first

I should be honest: feel-first design creates its own problems. The biggest one is scope — it's easy to cut anything that "doesn't serve the feeling," which can gut your game of interesting tension. Serene doesn't mean frictionless. wAv needed difficulty; the question was what kind.

The answer: difficulty through spatial reasoning, not pressure. The puzzle is in reading how the wave will behave — not in reacting faster or more accurately. That preserves calm while keeping the brain engaged.
