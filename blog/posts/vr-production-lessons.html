Lessons from Leading a VR Production (That Won an Award)

What Wentworth VR taught me about leading small creative teams, shipping under pressure, and the things that actually matter in interactive storytelling.

🥽

In 2018 I led development on Wentworth VR, an interactive cinematic VR experience based on the Australian TV drama. It won the Screen Producers Awards — Interactive / Game Production category that year. I'm proud of it. I'm also grateful for what it broke in me.

VR storytelling is a different problem

In traditional game design, you control where the player looks. In VR, you don't — and in cinematic VR, you really don't. The player is a presence in the scene, not a controller of it. That completely inverts the usual toolkit.

Most of our early design problems came from forgetting this. We'd design a dramatic reveal — and half our playtesters were looking the wrong way when it happened. The answer wasn't better design; it was humility. Guide attention with light, sound, and staging — not forced camera control.

Leading a small team under real constraints

We were a small team with a real deadline, a licensed IP, and stakeholders who understood film but not interactive media. That last point was the biggest challenge.

"Interactive" gets used loosely. To a film producer, interactive might mean "the player can choose ending A or B." To a game designer, it means something much richer. Bridging that gap — without either oversimplifying or overwhelming — took more communication work than any technical challenge on the project.

"The best thing I did as lead was define what 'done' meant before we started, in terms everyone could agree on."

What I'd do differently

Playtest earlier and with non-gamers. VR onboarding for someone who's never worn a headset is a completely different problem from VR onboarding for someone who plays games. We optimised for the latter and shipped with friction points for the former that we didn't fully resolve.

I'd also spend more time upfront on the sound design brief. In VR, audio is spatial — it does as much work as visuals for presence and direction. We treated it as a later-stage concern and paid for it in iteration time.

What carries forward

The core skill I built on Wentworth VR wasn't VR-specific. It was translating between creative disciplines — helping writers, cinematographers, and developers understand each other well enough to make decisions together. That's a skill I've used on every project since, and it's probably the one I'm most grateful for.
